Original activity guide

Original STEM activities for 4 year olds.

Five small experiments to try with a 4 year old at home. Each one gives the parent a quick setup, a simple thing to say, and one clear test the child can run.

How to use this page: Pick one activity, scan the quick start, and try the first loop. Stop while it is still fun. If it turns into free play after one prediction or redesign, that still counts.
Before you start: Keep pieces too large to mouth, keep ramps and towers low, point flashlights at the wall, and stay next to any water tray.
AgeBuilt for 4 year olds; adjust down for younger siblings.
Parent roleSet up the first version; let the child predict, test, and choose one change.
GoalGet one prediction, one test, and one redesign.
Stop ruleStop while it is still fun. Five good minutes count.

1 · ramps and force

Ramp Detective

A toy-car test where the child investigates which surface makes a car roll farthest.

Time5-8 minutes
Adult jobMake one low ramp and mark where the car stops with tape, socks, or blocks.
Stop afterTry two or three roads, then let it become car play.

Setup

  • Books or couch cushion for a low ramp
  • Flat cardboard or tray
  • Toy car
  • Towel, paper, or a placemat for surface changes

Read-aloud kid steps

  1. Roll the car down the plain ramp.
  2. Put one new road, like the towel or paper, on the ramp.
  3. Guess: will it go far, stop short, or get stuck?
  4. Roll once and mark where it stops.
Say this Which road will make the car go farthest: plain cardboard, towel, or placemat?
Rescue line If this turns into car play, do one last roll and say, "Let's mark this finish spot, then the cars can play."
Safety Keep the ramp low. No standing on books, trays, or couch cushions.
Watch for Does your child change one thing at a time, or do they turn it into free car play? Both are useful notes after one real roll.
Make it easier Use only two surfaces: plain cardboard and towel.
Make it harder Add tape marks and ask which road made the car stop farthest from the ramp.
Parent note

Notice: minutes engaged, favorite surface, whether the child made a prediction, and whether marking the stop spot made sense.

2 · engineering

Bridge Rescue

A bridge-building challenge where a toy has to cross a gap without falling into the river.

Time6-10 minutes
Adult jobStart with two books 4-6 inches apart and a paper bridge that can bend.
Stop afterTest one flat bridge and one stronger bridge, then let the story take over.

Setup

  • Two books
  • Paper, index cards, or one cardboard strip
  • Small toy animal or car
  • Blue towel or paper as the river

Read-aloud kid steps

  1. Put the toy on one book.
  2. Lay one paper bridge across the river.
  3. Try to drive or walk the toy across.
  4. If it bends, ask me to fold the paper lengthwise like an accordion, or add a second sheet.
  5. Try the stronger bridge once.
Say this The toy needs to cross the river. What bridge should we build first?
After it bends It bent in the middle. What could make the middle stronger?
Safety Use light toys only. Keep fingers out from under books, and do not stack books higher than one book per side.
Rescue line If the toy crashes on purpose, say, "One gentle rescue test first, then you can do a crash test."
Watch for Does the story help engagement? Does the child test gently or smash-test immediately?
Parent visual reference Watch this paper bridge example from Teach Beside Me first if you want to see the shape before setting it up.
Make it easier Start with a narrow gap and a flat cardboard bridge.
Make it harder Ask the bridge to hold two toys or cross a wider river.
Parent note

Notice: material chosen first, whether folding paper helped, and the exact phrase your child used when the bridge bent or failed.

3 · light and shapes

Shadow Builder

A flashlight-and-block activity where the child changes shadow size and shape by moving objects.

Time5-8 minutes
Adult jobDim the room just enough to see the shadow, set the flashlight on a stable surface pointing at a wall, and keep it still.
Stop afterMake one tiny shadow and one giant shadow.

Setup

  • Flashlight
  • Blocks, cups, or toy animals
  • Wall, door, or plain sheet
  • Painter tape for a shadow target, optional

Read-aloud kid steps

  1. Hold the toy in the beam of light.
  2. Move the toy close to the wall. Look at the shadow.
  3. Move the toy close to the light. Look at it grow.
  4. Make the shadow tiny, then giant.
Say this Can you move the toy, not the light, to make its shadow huge?
Safety Flashlight points at the wall only, never at faces.
Rescue line If the flashlight becomes the toy, give your child one turn to aim at the wall, then park it and move only the block.
Watch for Does the child understand moving closer/farther, or do they need your hand-over-hand demo?
Make it easier Use one block and only ask for big shadow, small shadow.
Make it harder Build a block tower and trace where its shadow lands.
Parent note

Notice: whether keeping the light still helped, if the child avoided shining in eyes, and which shadow change got the biggest reaction.

4 · structure and stability

Windproof Tower

A tower challenge where the child redesigns the base after gentle wind knocks it down.

Time6-10 minutes
Adult jobBe the wind for the first test with three slow fan waves.
Stop afterDo two rebuilds, then switch to free building.

Setup

  • Blocks, cups, magnetic tiles, or DUPLO
  • Paper plate or folded paper fan
  • Optional tape line for the tower spot

Read-aloud kid steps

  1. Build any tower.
  2. Stand back and watch the wind blow three times.
  3. Point to the part that fell first.
  4. Build again with a wider bottom.
Say this The wind is coming. How can we make this tower stay standing?
Safety Use light materials only. Keep towers below your child's chest.
Rescue line If the falling stops being funny, switch to: "Can we build one short tower that survives?"
Watch for Does your child naturally widen the base, lower the tower, or just rebuild the same tower?
Make it easier Build with cups; they fall dramatically but do not hurt.
Make it harder Add a rule: it must be taller than your hand and survive three gentle fans.
Parent note

Notice: material used, tower height, number of redesigns, and whether the three-wind rule kept failure funny or made it frustrating.

5 · water and buoyancy

Tiny Boat Cargo Test

A sink-or-float challenge where the child builds a small foil boat and tests how much cargo it can carry.

Time6-10 minutes
Adult jobPut a towel under the tray, add one inch of water, and shape the first foil boat.
Stop afterStop after the first big sink or one redesign.

Setup

  • Small sheet of foil, about the size of your hand
  • Shallow tray of water
  • Large cargo pieces like DUPLO bricks, counting bears, or chunky plastic lids too large to swallow
  • Towel under the tray

Make the boat

  1. Fold the foil edges up on all four sides.
  2. Pinch the corners so water cannot rush in right away.
  3. Keep the bottom wide and mostly flat.

Read-aloud kid steps

  1. Place the foil boat in the water.
  2. Make sure it floats on its own.
  3. Add one big block at a time.
  4. If it sinks, I will dump the water out. You pinch the sides higher, then we try one more passenger.
Say this Can your boat carry one passenger? What should we change before adding another?
Safety and mess One inch of water only. Use large cargo only, keep the towel under the tray, and empty the tray when the test ends.
Rescue line One sink is enough. After one redesign, empty the tray before it becomes splash play.
Watch for Does your child pinch the sides higher, flatten the bottom, or only keep adding cargo until it sinks?
Parent visual reference Watch this foil boat example from momkidhub first if you want to see the basic boat shape.
Make it easier You shape the first boat and let the child add cargo.
Make it harder Compare a flat boat, a bowl boat, and a long boat.
Parent note

Notice: cargo count before sinking, whether redesign happened, and how messy the water setup felt.

What to notice after trying one

  • Which activity got the fastest yes?
  • Which one lasted the longest?
  • Where did instructions break down?
  • What phrase did your child repeat or invent?
  • Which setup felt annoying enough that you would not repeat it?
